Taku korero mo te whakatoihara
- My story with inequality

By Kate Wise / Photo by Chris Wise / 02 OCT, 2023

This whole project began with a simple statement from my dad over the summer before my final year at university.
“When you go into your interview for a new job after uni, make sure you tell the interviewer you are infertile so he doesn’t use it against you that you might have children soon.”

“I a koe ka kōkiri atu ki tō uiui mō te mahi a muri i tō nohonga ki te whare wānanga, me matāra tō whakamōhio atu ki te kaiuiui, e puku pā ana koe. Kei kore te tangata e huri, te patu i a koe mā te kupu, ainī pea ka whakawhānau pēpī tonu.”

For his job he manages staff and conducts interviews for new hires too. This was his advice to me as a 32 year old married female with no children. Pretty grim stuff because I agreed wholeheartedly and even evaded answering the question of my family’s future plans in interviews I participated in after that conversation.

This is unfortunately just something most working women have faced. Whilst conducting research for the project I had several wahine tell me they believed they didn’t get the job based on one statement in their interviews - “Yes I have tamariki - children.” So why don’t men get asked these things? - Because children don’t often affect a male’s ability to work. Mostly, parenting falls on the mother, whether that is carrying the child or picking them up from school when they are sick. This is often due to them being the lower income earner - but that is a catch for another day.

So how could I take this conversation between my dad and I and turn it into a positive? Originally I conducted interviews and jotted down stories from other women with the intent to bring us all together through shared trauma - but that wasn’t helping the issue. The issue, as was concluded based on my findings, was that only one woman out of thirty had faced unequal behavior from another woman. The real problem here - was men.

Photo: Hane, Kate and Meriana.By Chris Wise, 2023.

Men were the ones causing these cultural differences in workplaces and piling up the workload of females until they needed eight arms to get all their extra work done. From event planning to workplace dishes, women accept extra work just because of their gender and in 2023, it’s time to change the way we think.

Eightarms was created to bridge the gap between your average kiwi bloke and the working wahine. We need all the help we can get and it starts with men recognising their actions and accepting change as a positive in their lives instead of a chore.

I whakatūria a Eight Arms hei arawhiti I runga I te whāruarua I waenganui I te tāhake kiwi kei tētahi taha me te wahine e mahi ana kei tāwāhi. Ka tino hiahia āwhina mātou, ā, ka tīmata taua āwhina ina ka mōhiohio te tāne ki ana ake mahi, ka whakaae hoki: he mea pai te panonitanga mā tōna oranga, ehara i te whakarīrā.

So how do we get their attention? Is it cliche to think that the average kiwi bloke likes fishing - yes? But so what? While researching I discovered this to be the most common hobby between men aged 30-40 and so the hoodwinking commenced.

Please don’t be mad that you were deceived with fishing themed puns and quirky illustrations - we are crying out for change and only you can make that happen. So cast your line out and reel in your biggest catch yet - equality for women.
